# Evaluation — Three Concepts, Compared

A side-by-side read of what each concept gets uniquely right, what it sacrifices, and who it lands hardest with.

---

## Concept A — Editorial Press

### What it gets uniquely right

- Establishes seniority **without flash**. The serif display, the fixed left rail of metadata, the editorial section heads under a 2px ink rule — every choice signals "this person has been operating long enough to have a written record."
- The **cribsheet → editorial article** mapping is the cleanest of the three. Credibility anchors live in the rail; the framing lives in the body. A reader who knows MIT Press Reader or Stripe Press recognizes the register immediately.
- The persona switcher reads as flipping to a chapter, not flipping a tab. The italic display serif and underline rule make the act of switching feel literary rather than UX-y.
- The micro-chart frontispiece is a quiet flex — a Pixxa-style nod that says <em>data is the byline here</em> without spelling it out.

### What it sacrifices

- Less suited to first-time visitors who need the *spectacle* of cinematography to grasp "Data Cinematographer."
- The serif body is comfortable but slightly less "modern" than a sans-led design — a deliberate choice that lands hardest with the reading audience and weakest with VC associates skimming for keywords.
- No animated demo of the data craft. Concept B owns that ground.

### Who it lands hardest with

- A senior CTO or founder who reads carefully.
- A board chair, partner-track VC, or HBS-trained operator looking for *evidence of seniority* over *evidence of energy*.
- A potential client who wants to be reassured before they email.

### Who it lands less well with

- Junior designers on Twitter.
- Marketing-led buyers shopping for "innovation."

## Concept B — Cinematic Lens

### What it gets uniquely right

- The site **is** the demo reel. A director-grade visitor instantly reads "Data Cinematographer" without reading the words.
- The **letterboxed hero with the animated scene chart** turns the home page into a directed sequence. This concept earns the phrase "Data Cinematographer" by *demonstrating* it.
- The **lens switcher** is a literal craft metaphor — five lens markers along a track, the active lens enlarged with a soft yellow focus ring. Reads instantly.
- The **dual-surface approach** (cinematic black for the home/persona/talks; warm off-white for case studies and writing) avoids the trap of being beautiful-but-unreadable.
- The **rack-focus page transition** (350ms blur-and-reveal on `<main>` mount) is the cinematic equivalent of a scene change. Confident motion, not fidgety.

### What it sacrifices

- Lands less obviously with a Modula-3-era systems audience who read the world in plain text. They are in Concept C's audience.
- The cinematic posture risks being read as "marketing-ish" by an HBS-trained reader if the cribsheet copy isn't rigorous — which is why the cribsheet copy was written first.
- More motion to maintain over time. Concept A and C will age with less effort.

### Who it lands hardest with

- A senior product or design lead who recognizes craft.
- A founder of a consumer-facing product who wants their data work to feel *directed*.
- An invited speaker / conference organizer (HarvardXR 2025 audience).

### Who it lands less well with

- A DEC SRC alum who reads everything in plain text and trusts only `man` pages.
- A buyer who needs to skim a deck in 30 seconds.

## Concept C — Systems Monograph

### What it gets uniquely right

- Lands hardest with the audience the brief points at: **DEC SRC alumni, Quantinuum-style CTOs, anyone who has read enough Knuth or Tufte to recognize the register.**
- The **Tufte-style sidenotes** handle the "knows about a lot of things" problem cleanly. Substance lives in the marginalia; the narrative lives in the column. The reader can stay in the prose or follow the citations as deep as they want.
- The **frontispiece** is the credibility anchor as visual evidence. The 1998 Critical Mass Inc. masthead — pulled from web.archive.org and shipped with the build — is an artifact nobody else can fake.
- **No motion** beyond a blinking terminal cursor on the frontispiece. Feels confident and scholarly.
- The persona switcher as an inline small-caps sentence ("READ AS — Product Builder · Data Storyteller · …") reads like choosing a chapter mode in a critical edition.

### What it sacrifices

- Risks reading as "academic" to a marketing-led buyer or junior designer.
- Requires excellent copy in the marginalia; if the sidenotes are thin, the layout collapses into a gimmick. (Currently the sidenotes are full; if they get edited later, that risk should be watched for.)
- The cream paper background and laboratory-green accent are the most niche aesthetic choice. A small, serious audience will love it; a broad audience will not understand it.

### Who it lands hardest with

- DEC SRC, MIT CSAIL, Bell Labs alumni.
- A Quantinuum CTO, a Modula-3 / SRC-influenced systems engineer, a Knuth reader.
- A board member of a research institute or a small-press founder.
- Any reader who knows what *Threads: A Modula-3 Systems Journal* was.

### Who it lands less well with

- A marketing-led buyer.
- A first-time visitor with 12 seconds of attention.

---

## Summary recommendation

If only one ships, **Concept A** is the safest hardest-working choice — it lands well across the widest senior audience without alienating anyone.

If two ship, ship **A and B**: A as the credibility surface, B as the linkable demo for the data-cinematography work and conference invitations.

If three ship, **Concept C** plays a niche but high-conviction role with the DEC SRC / Modula-3 / Tufte audience and probably routes that audience back into A or B depending on what they were looking for.

If four ship, **Concept D** is the strongest possible signal to the dataviz / illustration audience. It is the most visually distinctive of the set and will be the most-shared on social by anyone who recognizes RJ Andrews's lineage.

The four concepts are not four skins of the same site. They are four different bets about which reader the site is for.

## Concept D — Cartographer's Atlas

### What it gets uniquely right

- **The frontispiece earns the throughline.** A hand-drawn atlas with five labelled territories *shows* "I take vague ideas and turn them into things that actually run." The visual is the proof.
- **The persona switcher is a compass rose.** Choosing a persona feels like setting a bearing on a chart. No other concept makes the act of switching this physically meaningful.
- **Specimen cards as ribbon-banner plates.** Each project reads like a chromolithograph card from a 19th-century specimen book — instantly distinctive, and unfakeable without the same care for the typographic register.
- **Hand-drawn marginalia in Caveat.** Lets the site carry a *voice* in the literal margins — the operator narrating his own atlas — without compromising the rigour of the body type.
- **A completely different posture from A/B/C.** The four concepts as a set now span: editorial press → cinematic studio → research lab → draftsman's atlas. Genuinely four bets, not three plus a variant.

### What it sacrifices

- Reads as too whimsical for a CFO or compliance-led buyer. They belong to Concept A.
- Requires hand-drawn graphics to maintain over time. Adding a new persona means redrawing the frontispiece — deliberate friction, but real.
- The IM Fell English type is gorgeous but irregular. Some senior readers will want a cleaner sans on first glance; that audience is in Concept B.
- The current SVG frontispiece is a best-effort first plate; a final ship would commission a hand-inked replacement.

### Who it lands hardest with

- A **dataviz-literate** audience: RJ Andrews readers, *Information+* conference regulars, the *Beautiful Evidence* shelf.
- A **book-aware** audience: anyone with strong opinions about typography or printed-page design.
- A **creative-director or art-leaning founder** picking partners on craft signal.

### Who it lands less well with

- Compliance-led buyers, big-firm legal departments, or anyone scanning for "modern" cues to triage in 12 seconds.
- A reader who finds vintage register affected rather than confident.

---

## Updated summary recommendation

If only one ships: **A** (broadest senior reach).

If two ship: **A + B** (credibility + craft demo).

If three ship: **A + B + D** is a strong set — A for breadth, B for the linkable cinematography demo, D for the dataviz/illustration audience and the unmistakable signal. Concept C can be held back as a private link for the DEC SRC / Modula-3 audience.

If four ship, route by audience: A as the default surface; B for talks and conferences; D for the dataviz community; C for research-grade audiences.

If all five ship, **E** quietly becomes the most likely default. The Spectrum hero answers "what is this person" in a single chart and lands on the broadest audience without the warmth of A or the atmosphere of B/C/D — and so plays best as the canonical homepage with deep-links into the others.

## Concept E — Spectrum

### What it gets uniquely right

- **Answers the central problem with a chart, not a paragraph.** The Spectrum timeline shows five lanes of work spanning thirty-five years — the four-careers-stacked story is legible in five seconds.
- **Color encoding does real work.** Five categories, five hues, applied consistently across legend pills, timeline bars, project-card stripes, logo-wall stripes, and the closing-pitch border on each persona. The legend is the key to the whole site.
- **Builds on company logos.** Client wordmarks are the visual primary — typeset large in Inter 700 inside framed tiles, with a colour stripe under each name encoding the categories the work touched.
- **Modern register without affectation.** Clean Inter throughout; numerals tabular; no plate numbers, no banners, no ornament. Reads as contemporary information design (Pentagram / The Pudding / Reuters Graphics) rather than personal-brand kitsch.
- **Lands fastest of the five.** A 10-second visitor leaves with the right shape of the operator.

### What it sacrifices

- Less *atmospheric* than B (cinematic), C (scholarly), or D (atlas). Reads as a portfolio site, not a personal-brand object.
- Color encoding requires the visitor to learn it on the way in. Skim-readers can miss the meaning of the stripes.
- Adding a sixth category later requires a palette rework — the five hues are tuned to live together.
- The Spectrum's strength is comprehension, not seduction. A reader who wants warmth or voice will pick B or D first.

### Who it lands hardest with

- A modern enterprise buyer, a VC associate, a design-aware founder.
- Anyone who reads The Pudding / Reuters / FT data graphics.
- A first-time visitor who needs to grasp the operator in 10 seconds.

### Who it lands less well with

- A reader looking for warmth or atmosphere — they belong on B or D.
- A DEC SRC alum who reads everything in plain text — they belong on C.
